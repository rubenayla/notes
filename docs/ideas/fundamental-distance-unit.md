# A fundamental distance unit

A length unit defined **purely from fundamental physics**, with exactly one human choice: how many dozenal steps to climb to reach human scale. Everything else is fixed by nature.

## The definition

**unit = 12^N × ℓ_P**

where ℓ_P is the **Planck length** (≈ 1.616×10⁻³⁵ m) and **N is an integer** — the only free, human-chosen number. The unit is literally "N dozenal hops up from the Planck length." Because it's a pure multiple of a physical length, *stating* it needs no metre, no second, no frozen reference — only N and nature.

The guiding rule: **12 is principled, the Planck length is fixed by nature; the only arbitrary thing is the number of ×12 hops — nothing more.** (On base 12, see [Dozenal](dozenal.md).)

## Why the Planck length — the floor

The Planck length is the deepest length scale in physics, built from the constants of relativity, quantum mechanics, and gravity: ℓ_P = √(ħG/c³). But the *decisive* reason is structural: it is **(expected to be) the smallest length that physically means anything** — below it, the classical notion of distance breaks down.

So anchoring there gives the unit a **natural floor**: its "1" (N = 0, one Planck length) is the smallest meaningful length, and a length below 1 is physically impossible. No other anchor has this — an atomic-scale unit's "1" would float arbitrarily in the middle of the spectrum, with countless meaningful lengths beneath it.

*Honest caveat:* a minimum length is the strong, mainstream **expectation** from quantum gravity (the generalized uncertainty principle, discreteness in loop quantum gravity, a minimal length in string theory) — not an experimentally proven theorem. The floor argument rests on that expectation.

## No frozen numbers

The SI defines the metre via a *frozen* value of the speed of light, the kilogram via a frozen Planck constant, and so on. Those "exact" constants are conventions fixed by committee (2019) — fixing them created no physical accuracy, it merely *relocated* the uncertainty. Only **dimensionless** constants (the fine-structure constant α, mass ratios) are convention-free physics.

This unit uses **no frozen number.** It's a pure multiple of a real physical length; the only chosen input is the integer N. That is the cleanest a *dimensional* unit can be — you can't avoid leaning on physical constants to build one, but you can drive the arbitrary choices down to a single integer.

## The precision trade

The Planck length contains **G**, the gravitational constant — the **worst-measured constant in physics** (~2×10⁻⁵; gravity is too weak to measure precisely and offers no exact quantum effect to exploit). So the unit's *value in metres* is known only to ~5 figures, sharpening as G improves.

This is accepted deliberately: the **definition is exact**; only the *realization* is finite — honest, not faked, precision. And ~10⁻⁵ is already far beyond any human or engineering need (nobody measures a length better than parts-per-million), so the imprecision never bites in practice.

## Why not the precise (atomic) anchor

You could instead anchor to a precisely-known atomic length — the Rydberg length 1/R∞ is known to ~10⁻¹² (a million times better than the Planck length), and the electron is perfectly universal (identical everywhere, no less fundamental — the Planck length leans on G, itself a contingent free parameter). But the atomic anchor has **no floor**: atoms and nuclei sit far below it, so its "1" is arbitrary. Maximal fundamentality — the floor — is weighted over realizable precision.

### Cautionary example: the harmon

The existing dozenal [Harmonic System](https://dozenal.fandom.com/wiki/The_Harmonic_System) defines its length unit, the **harmon = 272.35 mm**, as (dozenal `1,001,700;`) ÷ Rydberg constant. A clean 12⁶ ÷ R∞ would give 272.09 mm — but the harmon nudges that by **+0.09%** so that 100 yards ≈ 240 harmons, a cubic half-harmon ≈ 2/3 US gallon, and so on. That deliberate fudge toward legacy units is exactly what this proposal forbids: the only freedom allowed is the integer hop, never a fitting factor.

## The human rung

The unit jumps by ×12 per step, so its human-usable rungs are fixed:

| N | size | hand reference |
|---|---|---|
| **31** | **4.60 cm** | finger, knuckle → tip |
| 31 − 1 step | 3.84 mm | finger crease mark |
| 32 | 55 cm | arm reach |
| 33 | 6.6 m | room |

The everyday unit is **N = 31 ≈ 4.60 cm.** The criterion: the everyday unit should not exceed a stretched hand span (~22 cm) and should stay within an order of magnitude of it — the hand is our manipulation scale, the thing always in view. N=32 (55 cm) exceeds the hand and feels awkward; N=31 is the only *fundamental* rung that fits under it. It's finger-scale, so the reference is a *part* of the hand (index finger, knuckle-to-tip), and its twelfth (3.84 mm) lands on a finger crease — unit and subdivision both map onto the same finger. Finger and thumb units have long historical precedent (the inch is a thumb; the "digit" a finger-width).

In these units a typical adult comes out: hand span ≈ 5, foot ≈ 6, reach ≈ 12 (one dozen), height ≈ 38.

No fundamental anchor places a rung *at* whole-hand size (~22 cm): the atomic family clusters its rungs at ~27 cm and ~2.3 cm (because α ≈ 1/144 = 12⁻²), Planck at ~4.6 cm and ~55 cm. A hand-sized rung would require a tuned multiplier — the forbidden fudge — so finger-scale is the honest best fit.
